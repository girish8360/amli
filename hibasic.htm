Introduction to Health Insurance

Health insurance is a financial protection mechanism designed to cover the cost of medical treatment arising from illness, accident, or hospitalisation. It works on the principle of risk pooling, where individuals pay a small amount called a premium at regular intervals, and in return, the insurance company agrees to bear a significant portion of their medical expenses as per the policy terms.

How Health Insurance Works (Basic Mechanism)

Under a health insurance policy, the insured person is covered for expenses such as:

  1. Hospitalisation (room rent, ICU, surgery, medicines)
  2. Pre-and post-hospitalisation expenses
  3. Day-care procedures
  4. Emergency treatments

    5. When a medical event occurs, the policyholder can either:

  5. Avail cashless treatment at a network hospital, where the insurer directly settles the bill, or
  6. Opt for reimbursement, where the insured pays first and later receives the eligible amount from the insurer.

Thus, health insurance converts an uncertain and potentially large medical expense into a manageable and predictable cost.

Importance of Health Insurance for a Middle-Class Family

For a middle-class family, health insurance is not a luxury but a financial necessity. Medical costs in India are rising rapidly due to:

  1. Expensive diagnostics and surgeries
  2. Increasing lifestyle diseases
  3. High cost of private healthcare

A single medical emergency can easily wipe out years of savings or force a family to take loans or sell assets. Health insurance plays a crucial role by:

  1. Providing Financial Security:It protects family savings from being exhausted due to unexpected hospital bills.
  2. Ensuring Access to Quality Healthcare:It allows families to seek timely and better medical treatment without worrying about affordability.
  3. Reducing Mental and Emotional Stress:During emergencies, the focus remains on recovery, not on arranging funds.
  4. Encouraging Preventive Care:Many policies also offer health check-ups and wellness benefits, promoting early detection of diseases.

Conclusion:In simple terms, health insurance acts as a financial safety net for a middle-class family. It safeguards income, protects long-term financial goals, and ensures that medical emergencies do not turn into financial crises. In today’s environment of rising healthcare costs, having adequate health insurance coverage is one of the most important and responsible financial decisions a family can make.

BASICS TERMS IN HEALTH INSURANCE

Take a look at some of the most common health insurance terms that you may come across:
  1. AYUSH Treatment: AYUSH treatment refers to medical treatments taken through Ayurveda, Yoga & Naturopathy, Unani, Siddha and Homeopathy system of medicines. Several health insurance plans cover AYUSH treatment costs
  2. Claim: Claim refers to the request made to the insurance company by the policyholder to pay the medical expenses incurred on an illness or hospitalization under the health insurance policy. In the absence of a claim, you will have to pay for the medical expenses on your own.
  3. Co-payment: Co-payment refers to a fixed percentage of the claim amount that the policyholder has to pay at the time of claim settlement. Opting for a co-payment can help to reduce your premium
  4. Coverage: Coverage refers to the extent of benefits available under a health insurance policy. The wider is the coverage, the more will be the benefits offered under the policy
  5. Cumulative Bonus: Cumulative bonus refers to an increase in the sum insured amount without a hike in premium as a reward for not raising a claim in the previous policy year
  6. Day Care Procedures: Day care procedures refer to those medical procedures and surgeries that are performed using advanced medical technology and require hospitalization of less than 24 hours. Almost all basic health insurance plans offer coverage for day care procedures. For example, cataract surgery
  7. Deductible: Deductible refers to a fixed amount that the policyholder agrees to pay towards the incurred medical expenses before raising a claim with the insurance company. It is a part of the total claim amount. Once the deductible is paid, the insurance company will pay for the remaining medical expenses claimed by the policyholder
  8. Dependent: Dependent refers to the family members of the policyholder who can also be covered under the same health insurance policy. It usually includes your legally wedded spouse, children, parents and parents-in-law.
  9. Domiciliary : Domiciliary treatment refers to the medical treatment taken at home under the supervision of a medical professional in case hospital admission is not possible. This treatment is covered by health insurance plans under domiciliary hospitalization
  10. Entry Age: Entry age refers to the age at which a person can buy a health insurance policy. Most health insurance plans come with an entry age of 91 days to 65 years. However 65+ years can be covered under NIVA BUPA
  11. Exclusions: Exclusions refer to the conditions or circumstances that are not covered under a health insurance policy. Any claim arising out of an excluded medical expense or circumstance is not payable by the Insurance Company
  12. Family Floater: Family floater refers to the type of coverage where a single sum insured amount is shared by all the insured family members on a floater basis. A family floater policy is more affordable than buying an individual policy for each family member.
  13. Free Look Period: Free look period refers to the first 30 days of buying the policy, where the policyholder can change the insurance company or cancel the policy without paying any cancellation fee. If the policy is cancelled during this period, then the premium is refunded to the policyholder
  14. Grace Period: Grace period refers to a fixed period that begins after the due date of a health policy. During this period, the policyholder can pay the due premium amount without losing the continuity benefits, such as waiting periods. Grace periods are usually of 15 days or 30 days.
  15. Indemnity Plan: An indemnity plan is a type of insurance policy where the claim amount is paid based on actual medical expenses incurred. Under this type of plan, the policyholder has to submit the medical bills to the insurance company so that they pay the claim amount equal to the total bill amount.
  16. Insured: Insured refers to the person who is eligible to receive medical coverage under a health insurance policy
  17. Insurer: Insurer refers to the insurance company that is responsible to pay for the medical expenses of the insured under a health insurance policy.
  18. Network Hospitals : Network hospitals refer to the empanelled hospitals of the insurance company that offer the cashless hospitalization benefit to the policyholders. All insurance companies in India have a network of cashless hospitals
  19. No Claim Bonus: No Claim Bonus is a renewal premium discount offered by insurance companies to policyholders for not raising a claim in the previous policy year. This discount can be accumulated up to 50% for five consecutive claim-free years.
  20. Portability: Portability refers to the procedure of changing the existing insurance company or health insurance policy without losing any continuity benefits like the waiting period. This facility is beneficial for people who are unhappy with their current insurer or policy.
  21. Pre-existing Diseases: Pre-existing diseases refer to the diseases or medical conditions that the applicant was diagnosed with up to 4 years before buying the health policy. Most health plans cover pre-existing diseases after 2 to 4 years of waiting period.
  22. Premium: Premium refers to the cost of an insurance policy. It is the amount paid by the policyholder at regular intervals to get insurance coverage and enjoy the benefits available under a health insurance policy.
  23. Preventive Health Check-up: Preventive Health Check-up Preventive Health Check-up refers to a series of medical tests that are undertaken to assess the health of a person and take suitable measures to prevent the occurrence of a disease
  24. Restoration Benefit: Restoration benefit refers to the facility of refilling your sum insured amount before the policy renewal date in case the original amount gets exhausted on raising one or more claims.
  25. Riders/ Add-on Covers: Riders or add-on covers refer to the additional covers that the policyholder can buy on payment of an extra premium amount to expand the coverage of a basic health insurance policy. For example, PED waiting period reduction, etc.
  26. Sub-limits: Sub-limits Sub-limits refer to the limit set on the coverage amount of a benefit under a health insurance policy. Eg: room rent limit. In case a coverage benefit comes with a sub-limit, the insurance company will only be liable to pay up to that limit, and any additional amount will have to be paid by the policyholder
  27. Sum Insured: Sum insured refers to the maximum coverage amount that the insurance company will pay in a policy year. The sum insured is ascertained at the time of buying or renewing the policy
  28. Top Up Plan: Top up plan refers to a type of health insurance plan that offers a higher sum insured and can be bought to enhance the medical coverage of a person. However, a deductible amount needs to be paid under all top up insurance plans, which makes its premium affordable.
  29. Underwriting : Underwriting refers to the process where an insurance company evaluates the application of a person. The underwriting team evaluates the medical history and personal details of a person to determine whether the policy should be issued and how much premium must be charged.
  30. Waiting Period: Waiting period refers to the time period from the commencement of the policy during which the policyholder is not allowed to make any claims. Any claims raised during this period will be rejected by the insurance company. For example, the PED waiting period, critical illnesses waiting period, etc.

DIFFERENCE BETWEEN
MEDICLAIM & HEALTH INSURANCE

Mediclaim and health insurance are two types of insurance products that provide financial protection against medical expenses. While they share some similarities, there are significant differences between them:

FEATURES MEDICLAIM HEALTH INSURANCE
Purpose Mediclaim policies are designed to reimburse hospitalization expenses, focusing on treating illnesses or injuries. Health insurance policies provide comprehensive coverage, including hospitalization, pre-hospitalization, post-hospitalization, and sometimes even wellness and preventive care.
Coverage Mediclaim policies typically cover hospitalization expenses, including room rent, doctor's fees, and medical expenses. Health insurance policies offer broader coverage, including:
  1. Pre-hospitalization expenses (e.g., doctor consultations, diagnostic tests)
  2. Post-hospitalization expenses (e.g., follow-up treatments, medication)
  3. Daycare procedures (e.g., cataract surgery, dialysis)
  4. Alternative treatments (e.g., Ayurveda, Unani)
  5. Wellness and preventive care (e.g., health check-ups, vaccinations)
Sum Insured Mediclaim policies usually have a lower sum insured, typically ranging from ₹50,000 to ₹5 lakhs Health insurance policies offer higher sum insured options, ranging from ₹2 lakhs to ₹1 crore or more.
Premium Mediclaim policies generally have lower premiums due to the limited coverage. Health insurance policies have higher premiums, reflecting the broader coverage and higher sum insured.
Tax Benefits Mediclaim policies are eligible for tax benefits under Section 80D of the Income Tax Act. Health insurance policies also offer tax benefits under Section 80D.
Waiting Period Mediclaim policies usually have a shorter waiting period for pre-existing diseases (typically 1-2 years). Health insurance policies may have a longer waiting period for pre-existing diseases (typically 2-4 years).
Renewability Mediclaim policies are usually renewable up to a certain age (e.g., 60-65 years). Health insurance policies are often renewable for a lifetime, with some insurers offering coverage up to 80-100 years.
Portability Mediclaim policies may not offer portability, or the ability to transfer the policy to another insurer. Health insurance policies usually offer portability, allowing policyholders to switch insurers while retaining accrued benefits.
DRAWBACKS OF MEDICLAIMS
Limited Coverage
  1. Hospitalization Expenses Only: Mediclaim policies only cover hospitalization expenses, leaving out other medical expenses like doctor consultations, diagnostic tests, and medication.
  2. No Coverage for Pre-Hospitalization Expenses: Mediclaim policies do not cover pre-hospitalization expenses, which can add up quickly.
Low Sum Insured
  1. Inadequate Coverage: Mediclaim policies typically have a low sum insured, which may not be enough to cover all medical expenses.
  2. Insufficient Coverage for Critical Illnesses: Mediclaim policies may not provide sufficient coverage for critical illnesses, which can result in significant out-of-pocket expenses.
No Coverage for Pre-Existing Diseases
  1. Waiting Period: Mediclaim policies usually have a waiting period for pre-existing diseases, during which no claims can be made.
  2. Exclusions: Some mediclaim policies may exclude certain pre-existing diseases or conditions altogether.
No Coverage for Daycare Procedures Exclusions: Mediclaim policies may exclude daycare procedures, which can result in significant out-of-pocket expenses.
Limited Renewability
  1. Age Limit: Mediclaim policies usually have an age limit for renewability, after which the policy cannot be renewed.
  2. Health Conditions: Some mediclaim policies may not be renewable if the policyholder develops certain health conditions.
No Portability No Transfer of Benefits: Mediclaim policies may not offer portability, which means that policyholders cannot transfer their accrued benefits to another insurer.
Limited Flexibility
  1. Inflexible Plans: Mediclaim policies often have inflexible plans that may not be tailored to an individual's specific needs.
  2. Limited Add-ons: Mediclaim policies may not offer additional coverage options or add-ons, which can limit their flexibility.
Higher Premiums for Older Adults Age-Based Premiums: Mediclaim policies often have age-based premiums, which can result in higher premiums for older adults.
Limited Coverage for Modern Treatments Exclusions: Mediclaim policies may exclude modern treatments like robotic surgeries, cyberknife treatments, or gene therapies.
Complexity Complex Policy Terms: Mediclaim policies can have complex policy terms and conditions, which can make it difficult for policyholders to understand what is covered and what is not.

Overall, while mediclaim policies provide some level of financial protection against medical expenses, they have several drawbacks that can limit their effectiveness.
In summary, while mediclaim policies provide basic hospitalization coverage, health insurance policies offer more comprehensive coverage, higher sum insured options, and additional benefits like wellness and preventive care.

What is Cashless Claim Settlement?

CASHLESS CLAIM SETTLEMENTIn a cashless claim, Niva Bupa directly pays the hospital for your treatment.
You only pay non-medical or uncovered expenses.This works only in network hospitals of Niva Bupa.
This facility allows a patient to be admitted to a hospital without paying any money upfront. If the hospital is in the insurer’s network, the patient can avail of cashless facility. This means that as per a predecided arrangement, the insurer will directly settle the bill with the hospital at the time of the patient’s discharge.

Cashless Claim Process (Step-by-Step)
For Planned Hospitalisation

  1. Choose a Niva Bupa network hospital.
  2. Inform hospital / Niva Bupa 48–72 hours before admission.
  3. Hospital sends pre-authorisation request to Niva Bupa.
  4. Niva Bupa approves → gives cashless approval amount.
  5. Get treated
  6. At discharge → you pay only non-covered items.
  7. Niva Bupa settles bill directly with hospital.

For Emergency Hospitalisation

  1. Get admitted immediately.
  2. Inform Niva Bupa within 24 hours.
  3. Hospital sends pre-auth.
  4. Same process continues.

Documents for Cashless Claim

Usually handled by hospital, but keep:

  1. Health insurance card / policy number
  2. Photo ID proof
  3. Doctor’s admission note
  4. Pre-authorisation form
  5. Discharge summary
  6. Final hospital bill

The Cashless Everywhere option is subject to the following conditions.

  1. For elective procedures, the customer should intimate the insurance company at least 48 hours prior to admission.
  2. For emergency treatment, the customer needs to intimate the insurance company within 48 hours of admission.
  3. The claim should be admissible as per the terms of the policy and the cashless facility should be admissible as per the operating guidelines of the insurance company.

    HEALTH CARE INFLATION IN INDIA
    IMPACT on MIDDLE CLASS FAMILIES
    An OUTLOOK for Next 5 YEARS
    (2025-2030)

    The Reality of Healthcare in India: India faces significant healthcare challenges, including:

    1. Limited Healthcare Infrastructure: India has a shortage of hospital beds and healthcare professionals, particularly in Tier-3 cities, towns & rural areas.
    2. High Out-of-Pocket Expenses: A significant portion of healthcare expenses in India is borne out-of-pocket, which can lead to financial distress for many families.
    3. Rising Medical Inflation: Medical inflation in India has been consistently higher than general inflation, making healthcare increasingly unaffordable

    THE RATE OF MEDICAL INFLATION IN INDIA

    As on today, here are the key recent data points:
    1. Several industry reports estimate medical inflation in India to be in the 10 % to 15 % per annum range.
    2. A specific figure says medical inflation “currently around 13.2%” in India, compared to general inflation of 4-6%.

    Why is medical inflation higher
    than general inflation:

    1. Increase in non-communicable diseases (diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular) → more treatments, more recurring costs.
    2. Rising cost of advanced medical technology, imported devices, diagnostics, newer therapies.
    3. Supply constraints: fewer beds, skilled professionals, infrastructure costs, which push up costs.
    4. Out-of-pocket burden: Many treatments are paid by households directly, which makes inflation felt more immediately

    Conclusion: A realistic working range for medical inflation in India currently is about 10 %–15% per year, with perhaps 12-14% being a reasonable mid-point assumption.

    Impact on a middle-class family
    over next 5 years

    In case of Middle-class families— Let's pick a baseline cost today and then project for 5 years using medical inflation.
    Example scenario: Suppose a middle-class family currently has average annual medical/healthcare cost (including hospitalisation + diagnostics + medicines) of ₹1,00,000 /-.
    Assume medical inflation at 12% per year (within the 10-15% range).

    Over 5 years, cost will grow by factor = (1 + 12%)^5 ≈ 1.12^5 ≈ 1.762 (i.e., ~76% higher).
    So in 5 years, annual cost would be ~ ₹1,76,000 (from ₹1,00,000).
    Example scenario: Suppose a middle-class family currently has average annual medical/healthcare cost (including hospitalisation + diagnostics + medicines) of ₹1,00,000 /-
    Assume medical inflation at 14% per year (within the 10-15% range).
    then factor is 1.14^5 ≈ 1.925 → cost ~ ₹1,92,500 (from ₹1,00,000)

    What it means: What you’re paying today (₹1 lakh) may nearly double (or at least increase significantly) in 5 years.

    1. Assume medical inflation at 12% per year-annual cost would be ~ ₹1,76,000/- (from ₹1,00,000)
    2. Assume medical inflation at 14% per year--annual cost would be ~ ₹1,92,500/- (from ₹1,00,000)

    For a middle family this means the “healthcare budget” has to grow, or else you will find yourself under-budgeting / underinsured.

    If incomes are growing at say 6-8% per year for many middleclass households,
    1. they will grow by maybe ~1.06^5 ≈ 1.34 (i.e., 34% growth) over 5 years.
    2. But healthcare cost could grow by ~76-90%.
      So healthcare cost growth > income growth → increasing burden

    This has implications for savings, insurance planning, health cover adequacy, emergency funds, etc.
    Additional risk features: Hospitalisation or major treatment costs: These get magnified. For instance, cost of some treatments has doubled in 5 years.
    Even if you have insurance, premium hikes and co-payments may increase.
    Coverage that looks adequate today may become insfficient in 5 years.
    Out-of-pocket expenses remain significant in India. So unless you plan ahead, you may face “bill shock”.

    What we should consider health cover (sum insured) keeping in mind that future healthcare costs will be higher.

    1. Choosing a sum insured that seems “comfortable now” may not be enough in 5 years.
    2. Consider top-up or super top-up covers (higher sum insured above a base cover) so that in case of major hospitalization the cover supports inflated costs.
    3. Encourage regular health check-ups, lifestyle improvements (prevention helps) – fewer major events means less burden of inflated costs.
    4. Build a healthcare buffer/emergency fund: Since some part may still be out-of-pocket, having savings for unexpected healthcare helps.
    5. Review and upgrade premiums/cover early rather than waiting. The younger and healthier you are, the better terms you’ll get.
      Waiting may mean higher loading and pre-existing conditions risk

    Simply inflation-adjusting with general inflation (say 6%) is insfficient; the healthcare inflation is much higher. So if we assumed cost doubling in 12 years (at 6%) we are underestimating.
    Given these factors, health insurance can provide financial protection and access to quality healthcare, ensuring that individuals and families can focus on recovery rather than worrying about the financial implications of medical emergencies.

    Riders & Add-ons
    (Available with plans)

    WHAT ARE RIDERS/AD-ONS:
    Riders (sometimes called add-on covers or endorsements) are optional enhancements you can attach to your base health insurance policy by paying an additional premium. They expand your coverage beyond what the basic policy offers.

    As of 2025, Niva Bupa (formerly Max Bupa) has introduced several popular riders and add-ons that customers can opt for to enhance their base health insurance coverage:

  • Personal Accident Cover – Provides a lump sum if the insured suffers permanent disability or death due to an accident.
  • Critical Illness Cover – Pays a lump sum on diagnosis of serious illnesses (like cancer, heart attack) covered under the rider.
  • Hospital Daily Cash – Pays a fixed amount for each day of hospitalization.
  • Safeguard and Safeguard+ – Covers small or non-payable items (like conveyance, oxygen, gloves) during treatment that aren’t normally reimbursed.
  • Disease Management / Acute Care Add-Ons – Provides extra benefits like tele-consultations, wellness programs, or removes waiting periods for certain diseases.
  • Zero Co-pay Add-On – Removes the co-payment requirement (so you don’t pay a share of the claim amount out-of-pocket) except for room category differences
    •  Why add riders?They let you tailor your cover to your needs — e.g., if you want maternity, cash benefit, or cover for specific chronic risks that the base plan doesn’t fully pay for.
    Note: Riders usually must be opted at purchase or renewal time, and not all can be added mid-term.

    EXCLUSIONS

    What Are Exclusions?
    Exclusions are situations, treatments, or conditions for which Niva Bupa will not pay claims under the policy. These are defined in your policy contract and are legal parts of any health insurance.

    MAJOR TYPES OF EXCLUSIONS

    A) Standard Exclusions (Common to Most Policies)

  • Pre-existing conditions — Illnesses you already had before buying the policy aren’t covered until waiting periods are over.
  • Initial waiting period — Most policies exclude claims for diseases occurring within the first ~30 days of policy start (except accidents).
  • Cosmetic or plastic surgeries — Procedures done for cosmetic/beauty reasons are not covered.
  • Dental or vision care — Routine dental/eye treatments are usually excluded.
  • Specific disease exclusions — Conditions like hernia, joint disorders, cataract, etc., have their own waiting period or can be excluded temporarily.
  • Self-inflicted injuries, drug/alcohol abuse, criminal acts — Any treatment resulting from these isn’t covered
  • War, terror, nuclear incidents — Treatment costs from such events are excluded
    •

    B) Permanent or Policy-Specific Exclusions
    Depending on the exact policy (like Health Recharge, ReAssure, Arogya Sanjeevani), there are further exclusions

  • Investigation & evaluation tests (routine tests without hospitalization)
  • Rest cure, rehabilitation, respite care
  • Weight control/obesity treatments
  • Change of gender procedures
  • Unproven/experimental treatments
  • Birth control, infertility treatments
  • Maternity (unless specific maternity benefit bought)
  • Domiciliary hospitalization & OPD treatment
  • Excluded hospital or unrecognized provider
  • Treatments outside India
  • Non-medical items (toiletries, convenience charges, etc.)
    •

    Waiting Periods vs Exclusions

  • Waiting periods are time-based exclusions (e.g., first 30 days; 24–36 months for specific diseases). During this time, claims for those conditions are not paid
  • Permanent exclusions never become payable under the policy (unless you buy a specific rider).
    •
